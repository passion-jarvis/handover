What to Delegate First: A Checklist for Business Owners at $10K+/Month

April 22, 20265 min readBy Jarvis VA

Most founders wait too long to hire. When they finally do, they hire without a plan — and the VA spends week one asking "what should I do?" Here's how to skip that part entirely.

The $10K threshold

We work with business owners doing $10,000 a month or more. That's usually the point where the founder is visibly drowning — not because the business is struggling, but because it's growing faster than they can personally manage.

At $10K/month, you're probably doing everything: responding to leads, handling client issues, managing your calendar, posting on social, chasing payments, doing your own books, and somehow also trying to find new customers. The business works. But it only works because you're holding every thread yourself.

That's the problem. A business that can only run if the owner is running it isn't a business. It's a job with extra steps.

The first thing to delegate is not the task you hate most.
It's the task that costs you the most when it's late.

The day-one audit: what to hand off first

Before the first VA call, we ask every client to do a 20-minute exercise. For every recurring task they do, they answer two questions: How long does it take? What happens if it's late or skipped?

The tasks with high time cost and high consequence when skipped — those go to a VA first. Not the ones you find annoying. The ones where delay costs you money or relationships.

Tier 1: Delegate immediately (day one)

Highest ROI, lowest risk to hand off
  • Lead follow-up and initial response — every hour you wait, conversion drops. A VA responding within 2 minutes keeps deals alive.
  • Calendar management and scheduling — not just accepting meetings, but protecting your time and declining the right things.
  • Customer inbox — ticket routing, standard responses, flagging what actually needs you. You don't need to see the inbox every day.
  • Invoice sending and payment tracking — not the strategy, just the execution. Did the invoice go out? Did it get paid? Is anything 7 days overdue?
  • Data entry and CRM updates — after every call or email, the CRM should update. You shouldn't be doing that manually.

Tier 2: Delegate by week two

Once your VA knows your business context
  • Social media scheduling and engagement — your VA posts content you approve, responds to comments, manages DMs.
  • Meeting prep — brief notes before each call, so you walk in already ready.
  • Vendor and supplier follow-up — chasing certificates, confirmations, deliverables that aren't your job to chase.
  • Client check-ins — the "just checking in" emails that keep relationships warm. They take 3 minutes each and your VA can send 20 a day.
  • Research tasks — competitor updates, prospect backgrounds, industry news you'd want to know about.

Tier 3: Delegate once systems are built

Higher trust, higher leverage
  • Bookkeeping — categorizing expenses, reconciling accounts, preparing monthly summaries for your accountant.
  • Content repurposing — turning your long-form content into clips, posts, and captions across platforms.
  • Hiring and onboarding support — screening applicants, scheduling interviews, building onboarding docs.
  • Reporting — weekly numbers, client performance reports, anything you currently build manually from raw data.

The mistake almost every founder makes

They hire a VA and then don't give them enough to do. They drip-feed tasks one by one, never quite letting go. After 30 days, the VA is underutilized, the founder is still drowning, and both sides are frustrated.

The fix: come into day one with a real brief. Not "help me with some stuff." A list of exactly which tasks transfer, what good looks like for each one, and what decisions the VA can make without asking.

The question to ask yourself right now

What did you do in the last 7 days that cost you more than an hour and didn't require you specifically to do it? Write that list down. That's your day-one delegation brief.

Most founders who do this exercise are surprised. The list is longer than they expected. And most of it can be handed off this week.
