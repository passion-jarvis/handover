The $300/Hour vs $20/Hour Mistake Every Founder Makes

April 22, 20265 min readBy Jarvis VA

Most founders doing $10K-$50K a month are walking around with a $300/hour brain spending 3-4 hours a day doing $20/hour work. Here's the math on what that costs you — and how to fix it this week.

First, figure out your actual hourly rate

Take your monthly revenue. Divide it by your working hours in a month. That's your effective hourly rate — what your time is actually worth when you're doing founder work.

A founder doing $30K a month, working 45 hours a week: their time is worth about $167/hour. A founder doing $50K a month at the same hours: $278/hour. These aren't salaries — they're the value generated per hour when you're focused on the work that actually moves the business.

Your hourly rate isn't what you charge.
It's what your business loses when you do $20/hour work.

The time audit most founders never do

Track everything you do for one week. Not the big projects — everything. Every email. Every time you log into Shopify to check an order. Every invoice you generate. Every follow-up you manually send. Every time you update your own CRM.

At the end of the week, go through the list and mark each task: Could a trained person do this for $20/hour? For most founders, 40-60% of their week falls into that category.

That's not theoretical. Every hour you spend on $20/hour work is an hour you're not spending on the things only you can do — closing new clients, building partnerships, creating the product, doing the work that compounds.

The tasks that are costing you the most

Based on what we see across 22 clients, here are the most common $20/hour tasks that founders are still doing themselves:

Customer inbox management

Most founders spend 1-3 hours a day in email. Responding to standard customer questions, processing refunds, answering "where is my order." A trained VA handles all of this. You read a daily summary. You stop being the inbox.

Bookkeeping and expense tracking

Categorizing expenses, reconciling accounts, chasing overdue invoices, preparing month-end summaries. A business coach we work with was spending 6 hours every week on this. That's 312 hours a year. His VA does it now. He gets Sunday nights back.

CRM and data entry

After every sales call or client interaction, updating the CRM, moving the deal stage, logging notes. These are 5-minute tasks that add up to hours when you're doing 10-20 of them a day. A VA does all of it as a standing workflow.

Lead follow-up

The leads that came in Monday but didn't book. The proposal that went out 5 days ago with no response. The prospect who said "I'll think about it." A VA tracks all of these and sends the right nudge at the right time. Manually, this either doesn't happen or takes 2 hours a week that you don't have.

Research and prep

Looking up background on a prospect before a call. Pulling market data before a client meeting. Summarizing a contract before you sign it. These are important tasks — but the research doesn't require your judgment, just your standards. Hand the brief to a VA, get the summary delivered.

The real cost isn't time. It's opportunity.

When you spend 3 hours on admin, you don't just lose 3 hours. You lose the compound effect of what those 3 hours could have built. The new client you didn't pitch. The partnership conversation that didn't happen. The product feature that's still on the whiteboard.

A general contractor we work with was spending 3 hours every Monday morning on subcontractor admin. After handing it to a VA, he used that time to close new bids. Revenue went from $3M to $4.2M in 12 months. The admin cost him more than money — it cost him the capacity to grow.

The $1,600/month question: A full-time Jarvis VA costs $1,600/month. If your hourly rate is $167 and you reclaim 15 hours a week, that's $2,505 in freed capacity every week. The VA pays for itself in 5 days. Every week after that is upside.

How to start this week

You don't need a perfect system before you hire. You need one task to hand over cleanly. Pick the task that takes the most time and requires the least of your specific expertise. Build the handoff for that one task. Give your VA ownership. Repeat.

Most founders who do this honestly say the same thing six months later: they wish they'd done it sooner. Not because the VA is magic — because they finally stopped being the one who handles everything.
