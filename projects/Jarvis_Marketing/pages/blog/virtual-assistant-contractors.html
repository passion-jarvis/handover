Virtual Assistant for Contractors: Stop Chasing Insurance Certificates Every Monday

April 22, 20265 min readBy Jarvis VA

General contractors run complex operations with dozens of moving parts. The problem isn't the work. It's the administrative overhead that eats your Monday before you've touched a single thing that grows the business.

The Monday morning trap

Ask any general contractor what Monday morning looks like and the answer is usually the same. Checking which subcontractors have sent updated certificates of insurance. Following up on unpaid invoices. Confirming that material deliveries are on schedule. Making sure the right crews are assigned to the right jobs.

These are not trivial things to skip. If a sub shows up on-site without a current certificate, you're liable. If an invoice goes unpaid long enough, the sub relationship strains. If materials don't show, the whole project timeline shifts. So you do it yourself, because nobody else is doing it right.

But that's exactly the problem. You're spending your highest-value hours on work that doesn't require your expertise — just your attention and follow-through.

3 hours every Monday.
That's 150 hours a year before you've touched a single new bid.

What a construction VA handles

Chases subcontractors for updated insurance certificates on a set schedule
Tracks unpaid invoices and sends reminders before they become disputes
Confirms material delivery schedules with suppliers
Prepares project status summaries for weekly reviews
Handles permit application paperwork and deadline tracking
Responds to client inquiries and keeps project communication organized
Prepares bid documentation and follows up on outstanding proposals

Also automated via Durable: Certificate expiry date logged → reminder sent to sub 30 days before expiry → if not received by expiry date → escalation alert sent to VA → VA follows up manually until received.

What he did with those 150 hours

He didn't use them for more admin. He used them for site visits and closing new bids. Those are the activities that directly produced the $1.2M in additional revenue. The VA didn't generate the revenue — the contractor did. The VA just cleared the path.

That's the pattern we see across every construction client. The work that grows the business — relationships, bids, site presence — always gets squeezed out by the admin work that has to happen. A VA doesn't replace the contractor. It removes the reason the contractor can't do more of what they're actually good at.

The liability angle most contractors miss

There's a risk management benefit that's easy to overlook. When certificate tracking is manual and informal, things fall through. A sub works on-site with an expired certificate. Something happens. You're exposed.

When a VA owns the tracking system with automated reminders and a clear escalation protocol, nothing falls through. Every sub is either current or flagged before they're on-site. That's not just time savings — it's liability reduction built into your operations.
