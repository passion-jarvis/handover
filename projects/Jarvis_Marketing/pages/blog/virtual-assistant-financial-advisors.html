Virtual Assistant for Financial Advisors: How to Double Your Client Review Meetings

April 22, 20266 min readBy Jarvis VA

Financial advisors get paid for their expertise, not their prep work. But most advisors are spending hours every week preparing for meetings that a trained VA could prep in 45 minutes. Here's what that costs you — and how to get it back.

The prep problem

Every client review meeting has a fixed overhead: pull the account summaries, review the notes from the last meeting, update the CMA, print or prepare the agenda. On average, this takes 2 hours per meeting for a thorough advisor.

If you're doing 3 review meetings a week, that's 6 hours of prep. That's 6 hours you're not spending on prospecting, deepening relationships, or building the business. Over a year, that's more than 300 hours of prep work.

The insight: prep doesn't require an advisor's judgment. It requires an advisor's standards. The moment you can transfer your standards to a trained VA, the prep time transfers with them.

The capacity math

Going from 3 to 7 review meetings a week isn't just a schedule change. It's a capacity change. If each review meeting has even a small upsell opportunity — additional assets, referrals, new product conversations — doubling your meeting volume can directly multiply your AUM growth over a year.

The math is simple: at $50M AUM with a 1% advisory fee, every $5M in new assets is $50,000 in additional annual revenue. If doubling your meeting capacity helps you close even one additional referral per month, the ROI on the VA investment is measured in multiples, not percentages.

What about compliance?

This is the first question most advisors ask. A Jarvis VA handles administrative and preparation tasks — not advice, not investment decisions, not client-facing regulatory communication. Your VA preps the brief. You deliver the meeting. The compliance boundary is clear.

Many advisors also find their VA helps with compliance documentation — tracking deadlines, organizing form submissions, ensuring nothing falls through the cracks before an audit. The VA doesn't make compliance decisions. They make sure you never miss a deadline because it was buried in your calendar.
