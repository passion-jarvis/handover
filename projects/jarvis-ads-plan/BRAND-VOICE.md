# Brand Voice Guidelines
## Jarvis (gojarvis.ai)
### Analysis Date: 2026-04-09

---

## Voice Summary

Jarvis speaks to burnt-out founders with the directness of a drill sergeant and the empathy of someone who's been there. The voice is confrontational but not hostile — it names the exact pain the reader is feeling, makes the cost of staying stuck feel real and expensive, and then offers a clean exit. Every sentence earns its place. The brand never wastes words, never softens bad news, and never lets the reader off the hook.

---

## Voice Dimensions

### Formal <-----> Casual: 4/10

Jarvis leans casual but never sloppy. Contractions are standard ("you're", "it's", "you'll"). The brand addresses founders directly as "you" throughout. Sentences are short and punchy, not academic. But there's a controlled, professional edge — no slang, no emoji in website copy, no filler phrases.

**Evidence:**
- "Yet here you are — answering emails at 11pm, doing work any trained VA could handle."
- "You work ON the business. Your VA runs the ops engine."
- "3 questions. 60 seconds. Find out if Jarvis is right for you."
- "Screened, trained, AI-equipped and ready in one week. Not months. One week."
- "The only risk is staying stuck."

---

### Serious <-----> Playful: 2/10

Jarvis is not playful. The brand takes the founder's time and money extremely seriously — and treats every wasted hour as a real loss. There's dark humor embedded in the framing ("Your business became your prison. We have the key."), but it's gallows humor, not lighthearted. No emojis in web copy. No puns. No exclamation point abuse.

**Evidence:**
- "YOU DIDN'T START A BUSINESS TO WORK 60-HOUR WEEKS."
- "Still at your desk." (11:47pm visual)
- "Your big ideas are collecting dust."
- "Fires are put out before they even happen."
- Footer: "Stop doing $10/hr work." (No punctuation softening. Just a command.)

---

### Technical <-----> Simple: 8/10

Jarvis avoids all jargon. The value proposition is explained in plain language anyone can understand. Complex ideas (opportunity cost, delegation, VA vetting) are made concrete through examples and math — not industry-speak. The brand assumes the reader is smart but busy, not technical.

**Evidence:**
- "3 hrs daily admin × $200 your hourly rate × 20 working days = $12K lost per month"
- "Only 1 out of every 200 VA applicants passes our tests."
- "Simulation-tested before they touch your business."
- "Email Management · Calendar Scheduling · Customer Follow-ups" (scrolling ticker — plain English tasks)
- "You Talk to a Human. The AI Does the Work." (no tech jargon despite AI reference)

---

### Reserved <-----> Bold: 9/10

This is Jarvis's defining dimension. The brand makes aggressive, direct claims without hedging. Headlines scream. The math proves a point and dares you to argue. The before/after framing doesn't soften — it confronts. "You are the bottleneck." is not a gentle observation. Neither is "Stop paying yourself $10/hr."

**Evidence:**
- "YOUR TIME IS WORTH $200/HR. SO WHY ARE YOU DOING $10/HR WORK?"
- "Your business became your prison. We have the key."
- "The only risk is staying stuck."
- "You think no one can do it better than you." (calling out a founder belief directly)
- "STOP PAYING YOURSELF $10/HR." (quiz result headline)
- "You think like a CEO again." (implied: right now, you don't)

## Brand Personality

**Primary Archetype: The Rebel**
Jarvis challenges the status quo — the idea that founders should be doing everything themselves. The brand is provocative, confrontational, and deliberate about making the reader uncomfortable with their current situation. It doesn't play it safe. Headlines are all-caps, declarations, challenges.

**Secondary Archetype: The Authority**
Behind the rebel framing, Jarvis backs every claim with numbers. "4.9/5 founder rating." "200+ founders freed." "Only 1 out of 200 VA applicants passes." "15 hours back per week avg." The authority is the proof layer that earns the right to be that bold.

**Archetype fit: Strong.** The rebel+authority combination is rare and effective. Most VA companies are bland and corporate (guides, friends). Jarvis occupies unoccupied territory.

## Vocabulary

### Words We Use Frequently

**Action words:**
- Scale, delegate, grow, free, reclaim, build, run, focus, handle, manage, streamline

**Descriptive words:**
- Elite, reliable, pre-trained, simulation-tested, background-checked, AI-equipped, verified

**Pain words:**
- Burnout, chaos, prison, stuck, bottleneck, fires, grind, constantly, putting out

**Value words:**
- Freedom, focus, growth, motion, vision, CEO

**Financial framing:**
- $10/hr work, $200/hr rate, opportunity cost, 15 hours back, $0 upfront, investment

**Time language:**
- Week 1, 60 seconds, 7 days, 15hrs, 60-hour weeks, 11pm, "not months — one week"

### Words We Avoid
- "Price" (not in sales contexts — use investment or cost)
- "Cheap" or "affordable" (signals value erosion)
- "Virtual assistant service" (too generic — Jarvis is a matching + automation system)
- "We believe" / "We think" / "May help" (hedging is not Jarvis)
- Corporate phrases: "synergy", "leverage", "bandwidth", "circle back", "touch base"
- Passive voice: "Assistance can be provided" → "We handle it."
- Superlatives without proof: "the best" without evidence

### Signature Phrases

| Phrase | Where used | Function |
|---|---|---|
| "Scale without burnout" | Homepage hero | Core tagline |
| "Stop doing $10/hr work" | Footer, LP2 | Persistent identity statement |
| "You didn't start a business to..." | LP1 hero | Empathy + challenge opener |
| "Your VA is ready." | Quiz result | Permission + urgency |
| "200+ founders freed" | Trust badges | Social proof shorthand |
| "$0 upfront" | CTAs, microcopy | Risk removal |
| "Work ON the business, not in it" | LPs | Michael Gerber callback, understood by target audience |

**Linguistic patterns:**
- Short declarative sentences. Period. Often incomplete.
- All-caps for emotional peak statements.
- "Not X. Y." contrast structure (e.g., "Not months. One week.")
- Numbers before nouns: "15 hours back", "200+ founders", "4.9/5"
- Second person throughout: "you", "your" — never "business owners" in copy
- Before/After framing as a structural device, not just a content choice

## Writing Guidelines

### Do's

- **Lead with the founder's pain, not our product.** Start from their world, not ours.
- **Use second person throughout.** Every piece of copy talks to "you" — not "business owners" or "clients."
- **Name the exact behavior.** Not "spending too much time on tasks" — "answering emails at 11pm."
- **Use math to make cost tangible.** If a founder wastes 3 hrs/day, show them the $12K/month they're losing.
- **Make the stakes real before the solution appears.** Pain first, then relief.
- **Be specific with proof.** Not "many clients" — "200+ founders." Not "fast" — "7 days."
- **Use short sentences for impact.** Then vary with a longer explanatory sentence when needed.
- **Contrast before/after in binary terms.** The gap between "Before Jarvis" and "After Jarvis" should feel dramatic.
- **Use "investment" not "price" when discussing cost.**
- **Let the call-to-action do one job.** "Get my time back →" is better than "Click here to learn more."
- **All-caps is a tool, not decoration.** Reserve it for the single most important phrase on a page.
- **End with a statement, not a question.** "Your VA is ready." not "Are you ready for a VA?"

### Don'ts

- **Don't hedge.** "We believe our VAs may be able to help" → Never. Remove all qualifiers.
- **Don't pad.** If it can be said in 5 words, don't use 10.
- **Don't start with "We."** Start with "You" or the pain point. The brand is not the hero — the founder is.
- **Don't list features without context.** "Email management" means nothing. "Your inbox, answered and filtered while you sleep" is real.
- **Don't use passive voice in CTAs.** "Get started" → weak. "Get my time back" → strong.
- **Don't oversell with adjectives.** Let numbers do the heavy lifting. "15 hours" beats "massive time savings."
- **Don't mix tones in the same piece.** If the headline is confrontational, the CTA can't be timid.
- **Don't use more than one exclamation point per page.** Overuse kills urgency.
- **Don't explain what a VA is in detail upfront.** Target audience knows. Get to the benefit.
- **Don't use generic testimonials.** "Jarvis helped my business!" → useless. "My revenue grew 2x and I took a 16-day vacation." → real.

Week 1: Your inbox is managed. Week 2: Your calendar runs without you. Week 3: Your first SOP is built and documented. Week 4: You're working ON the business. Your VA is running the ops engine. You have 15 hours back.

| Dimension | Jarvis | Belay Solutions | MyOutDesk | Time Etc |

| Formal <> Casual | 4/10 (casual) | 6/10 (semi-formal) | 5/10 (neutral) | 7/10 (formal) |
| Serious <> Playful | 2/10 (serious) | 4/10 (friendly) | 3/10 (serious) | 5/10 (friendly) |

| Primary Archetype | Rebel + Authority | Friend | Guide | Guide |

**Differentiation Assessment:**
- Belay, Time Etc, and MyOutDesk all sound like helpful guides or friendly service providers. Warm, professional, safe. Jarvis is the only one in the space that confronts the founder's role in their own problem.
- The "rebel with receipts" combination (bold claims backed by hard numbers) is completely unoccupied by competitors.
- Nobody else in VA services makes cost of inaction the centerpiece of their messaging. "You're paying yourself $10/hr" is a unique angle — use it everywhere.
- **Unoccupied voice territory:** Urgency + math. No competitor talks in dollar-per-hour opportunity cost. Own it.

### Immediate Actions

1. **Create a one-page voice card** — pull the Voice Chart + Do's/Don'ts into a single-page PDF or Notion doc every team member can reference before posting or writing.
2. **Audit Instagram captions** against this framework. If Kamille is writing them, she needs this document before her next post.
3. **Standardize email subject line voice** — every email should sound like it came from the same brand as "Stop doing $10/hr work."
4. **Apply the "cost of inaction" math frame** to all new ad copy. This is the most differentiated angle in the market — it should appear in every cold ad hook.
5. **Add "you are the bottleneck" framing** to sales call scripts for George — it's the sharpest articulation of the pain and it's already in the website copy.

### Voice Evolution Opportunities

1. **Build social proof into the voice itself.** The brand has real results ("2x revenue + 16-day vacation"). Specific founder wins told in this voice — short, punchy, named — would amplify credibility without softening the tone.
2. **Test a warmer welcome sequence** for new clients that maintains boldness but shifts from "confrontational" to "you made the right call" energy. The current voice is strong for acquisition; retention messaging can flex slightly warmer.
3. **Develop a distinct Jarvis "content voice"** for LinkedIn and Instagram that's punchy thought leadership — same worldview, lighter on product, heavier on founder pain observations.

### Consistency Improvements

1. **Email templates** — every GHL follow-up sequence needs to be rewritten in this voice. Default CRM copy sounds nothing like Jarvis.
2. **Onboarding comms** — Ruth's client-facing messages should match this framework. Right now it's likely inconsistent.
3. **Instagram bio audit** — needs to lead with the pain/benefit, not a description. Suggested: "You built a $10K+/month business. Now stop running it alone. ↓"
4. **Booking confirmation copy** — audit the Calendly confirmation email. Most of the time it sounds generic. It should sound like Jarvis.
